Campus Fund... ee 


Although the Campus Fund last ‘year collected more money 
than it did the previous year, it still fell short of its goal of 
$3500. Apathy. among the students is one reason for not reaching 
the goal, and the lack of experience among its workers is another. 
Last year the co-chairmen and most of the other workers had 
little or no previous experience with the Fund. We suggest that 
the Chapel Board consider the following suggestions to strengthen 
the annual drive : 


~1. Sophomore and Junior Co-Chairmen. The Campus Fund 
would be more efficient with co-chairmen, one from the sophomore 
class and one from the junior. Under this plan the junior chair- 
man, Waving been trained the previous year, could be the primary 
leader of the drive and could train the sophomore to take his 
position the next year. The junior.chairman, in addition, would 
still be at College the following ¥ear and could advise the new co- 
chairmen, . ‘ 


2-Fund Workers from the Underclasses. The Campus Fund 
‘ would be wise to draw most of its workers from the underclasses. 
In the first place, men from these classes could be trained during 
their freshman and sophomore years and would be better able to 
“handle publicity, treasury and solicitations when they are juniors. 
©’ And secondly, underclassmen would be more reliable workers than 
apathetic seniors who have positions on campus but who have 
little interest in. the Fund itself. 


3. More Suitable Date. Last year,the Fund drive took place the 

. third week in February. This date, although satisfactory then, will 

now conflict with the fraternity visitation of the Deferred Rushing 

program. Since there is no Hell Week early in the second semester 

under the new rushing program, the Fund should consider schedul- 

ing its drive the first week of second semester, This date would 
not conflict with hour exams or student activities. 


4. Suitable Charities. The Campus Fund has often selected 
charities which are controlled directly or indirectly by Protestants. 
Since the Fund is controlled by the Chapel Board, this type of 
selection is understandable; but about. one third of Hamilton’s 
students are not Protestants. The Fund should either select secu- 
lar charities exclusively or else contribute to groups from all three 
major religions. The Fund, in addition, should emphasize the fact 
that students with particular interests may specify charities other 
than those sponsored by the Fund. 


5. Follow Up Program. lf the Fund were staffed by under- 
classmen and by trained leaders, the co-chairmen would no longer 
have to finish up other major jobs and thereby would have more 
time to see that all solicitations were made and to contact those 
students who had refused to contribute. A follow-up program 
would probably yield several hundred dollars more from those 
students and faculty member who had been missed or who pre- 
viously had not cared to contribute. 


The Campus Fund may suffer from lack of experience this 
year, even if it adopts these suggestions. But if the program 
outlined here is followed, the Fund should nevertheless do better 


than the last three years and would make a good start toward » 


developing an efficient, trained organization.

by ScoTr PERRY 

Hamilton College needs a 
theater. 

President Robert W. McEwen 
has said that a theater is next 
on the building ‘program. The 
question remains, what sort of a 
theater? 

Prof. Edward C. Cole, who 
spoke jin Chapel Wednesday 
night on Building for the Ren- 
aissance in the Modern Theater, 
has been a technical consultant 
in the erection of several mod- 
ern theaters, among them the 
Shakespeare festival theater at 
Stratford, Connectitcut. 

Vintage Theater 

“Just as a 1925 vintage physics 
lab is no longer adequate on a 
college campus of 1960, so is a 
1925 vintage theater inadequate 
on a modern campus,” said Prof. 
Cole. He did not discuss the ad- 
aduacy or inadequacy of no the- 
ater at all. 

Prof. Cole said that the single 
most important aspect of the 
modern theater is its flexability. 
This is even more true, he said, 
on a campus such as Hamilton’s 
where production demands are 
varied. Theater flexibility is 
necessary if “full advantage of 
the three dimensional space 
available” is to be made. 

Many small colleges have build- 
ings designed to cover all of 
their fine arts needs—not only 
drama, but painting and music 
as well. 

Adjust to Plays 


The theater itself, Prof. Cole 
said, must be designed to adjust 
to plays in the round or plays 
given under the traditional pros- ' 
cenium arch, the stage familiar 
to all as a room with one wall 
removed. : 

He said that playwrights are 
no longer bound by the rigid 
conventions of the past, such as 
confining all the action in a play 
to one location, so that there is 
no need to continue building 
theaters as they have been built 
for centuries with the audience 
and actors separated from one
